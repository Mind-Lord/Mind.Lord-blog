When it went all wrong

My self-improvement journey started when I joined the gym. It all changed then; the reason made me hit rock bottom. Now, I prefer pain because it's the realest feeling. What about you?

First started

I joined the gym in March of 2022. I turned 15 in May. I felt the most amount of pain because it never happened before;
I was hiding my real feelings from everyone. I was at rock bottom, the worst of humankind. I felt that I needed to take my vengeance.
I was so focused and motivated that I barely skipped workouts; nothing else mattered anymore. I wanted to become the best.
I was a beginner and didn't know how to train hard, so I spent the first 10 months understanding how to train well enough to start my comeback.
The first 1.5 years weren't bad; I increased my strength and was able to achieve failure more often.
I know that exercise scientists will criticize me, but I worked out for about 4 hours just because I wasted time talking to gym bros and rested between sets without a timer.
It sounds strange and unnatural, but it worked even with highs and lows. The 1.5 years I spent at my first gym were really good, but then...

The tragic year at my second gym

After an argument with the owner of the first gym, I left and searched for a new gym that could satisfy my needs.
In Italy, the term 'gym' means a place of a minimum of 40 square meters with some machines, dumbbells, and other stuff.
After visiting two gyms that weren't up to American standards, I went to one that was meant to be a functional training gym, but there are:
3 benches, 2 rack for squat without safety bars and Technogym's machines like 1 leg extension, 1 seated leg curl, cables, 1 leg press, 1 lat machine, 2 treadmills, 1 cyclette and 1 elliptical machine.
It's the definition I gave you above of what 'gyms' are in Italy. My dad pays a monthly fee of €70 for a gym that has a value of €30.
The owner isn't the same as the other one, who was just greedy; the fee was €60, but the value was the same.
So here we are, in a gym that I didn't like from the beginning.
There weren't people who knew how to train, and they weren't people who would sacrifice some blood in the gym.
The people who knew how to train went to other gyms. I don't care if I am the only one slamming weights and screaming when achieving failure.

Little anecdote from the first gym

After I had been at the gym for a year, I had a heavy leg session, which meant heavy squats.
I have very strong legs compared to my upper body's strength.
I loaded my usual 95kgs (209lbs) and started my descent to parallel for, I think, 6 to 8 reps.
But then I couldn't get up, and the barbell fell on the safety bars.
My head felt heavy, and during the set, I was about to throw up; it was just rushing up my throat.
The very next day at school, I had nosebleeds at least twice a day. I thought, 'F*ck, I love it.'
I wasn't happy about the nosebleeds—they were the annoying part.
What I loved was the fact that it happened because of the heavy squat session.
The negative part was that I couldn't lift heavy for the next 3 months.
But when I didn't have nosebleeds for 2 days, I lifted heavy again, and then the nosebleeds returned.
I did this for about 5 months and then stopped because I was really tired of the nosebleeds.
I just took some vitamin C supplements prescribed by the doctor.

What supplements do I take?

The answer to this question is: None. I don't take supplements.
My nutritionist says that food is all I need, and for someone who trains 3x a week, supplements like protein powder, amino acids, vitamins, etc., are not needed.
Maybe in a stalemate, he will give me at least creatine monohydrate.
In the bulk phase, which we started in February, maybe he could change something because in May, I couldn't eat as much as I did 3 months before—I felt immediately full.
In June, we started my first cut after the first bulk. I still train abs when I remember, but I don't know my BMI because the next check is on September 3rd.
I just know that I've lost 2kgs, and I hope it's only fat.
Dieting is the simple part of the game, but sometimes it's just hard to take another bite.
I can't understand the thing I'm going to explain in the next paragraph. Keep reading.

The highs and lows

It happened in my first year and still happens, but I can't understand why I lose muscle mass and how I gain it.
I know all the processes, but I don't understand when one happens and when the other does, even if I do the same things every time.
I mean, I try to achieve failure or near it on a lot of exercises.
On a good part of them, I can, and on the last exercises, I feel so tired that doing another exercise seems impossible.
I don't have DOMS, and I know that it doesn't necessarily mean I've trained well, but simply I don't understand why I lose muscle or gain it.
Can someone explain it to me on my Instagram*?

*overall_small

The actual reason of the tragic year

I know I should have written it in the paragraph above, but other things rushed to my mind.
In a few words, after I joined the new gym for 6 months, I lost all the muscle mass I gained in the 1.5 years—it was about 3 to 4 kgs.
I wanted to change it immediately, but then I spoke with one of the guys who worked there.
I told him that if I didn't gain the muscle mass I lost by June, I would change gyms.
He was the one who trained harder than anyone.
So from February to June, the rhythm changed, and I could gain back the muscle mass I lost. Not every pound, but I gained it back.
This was the last time I would go to a gym where for half a year, I lose everything, and which qualitatively isn't better than the previous one.

Last things

It has been 2.4 years since I joined the gym, and I have experienced a lot of things.
Maybe the last is a great injury that I prefer not to ever have because mentally, it would be devastating. I don't want to stop.
I can keep training if I break an arm or a leg. I can keep training if I break something that is useless during training.
I don't care; I won't stop.
Self-improvement f*cked my mind up in a positive manner, and I can't imagine going back to who I was before it.
